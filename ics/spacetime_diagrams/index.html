Spacetime Diagrams
by S. Park & A. Rodriguez Certified Tier 2 6 citations

Research Question

How can a two-dimensional diagram capture both space and time — and what does a moving observer's tilted axis reveal about the geometry of simultaneity?

Explainer

In ordinary life we draw maps with two spatial axes (x and y). Spacetime diagrams do something stranger: they plot position on the horizontal axis and time on the vertical, so that a single point represents not a place but an event — something that happens at a definite place and a definite time. The worldline of an object is the curve it traces through this diagram as time passes.

A stationary object sits at the same x-coordinate for all time, so its worldline is a vertical line. An object moving at constant speed v has worldline slope Δ(ct)/Δx = c/v = 1/β. We use ct rather than t on the vertical axis so that both axes have units of length and the diagrams scale nicely.

Light has the special property that its speed is c in every inertial frame (Einstein's second postulate). In the diagram, a light ray always has slope 1 — a 45° line. This is the light cone. No massive object can travel along or inside the light cone; every material worldline must be steeper than 45° (slope > 1).

Now suppose frame S' moves at speed v relative to S. What does the diagram look like for an observer in S'? Their time axis (ct') tilts toward the light cone by angle θ = arctan(β) from the vertical. Their space axis (x'), which represents their lines of constant t', tilts upward by the same angle from the horizontal. Both axes squeeze toward the 45° light cone together. This asymmetric tilt is the geometric signature of the relativity of simultaneity: two events on the same horizontal line (simultaneous in S) fall on different t'=const lines in S', so they are not simultaneous in S'.

As β increases toward 1, both axes tilt more and more until they nearly coincide with the light cone at β = 1. This is why nothing with mass can reach the speed of light: at c, the time and space axes collapse onto each other and the coordinate system becomes degenerate.

Verification

Limiting Case 1 — β = 0 (frames at rest relative to each other)

Expected: When v = 0, S and S' are the same frame. The ct′ axis should be vertical (identical to ct) and the x′ axis should be horizontal (identical to x). No tilt.

Observed: Setting β = 0 in the simulation shows the primed axes perfectly overlapping the unprimed axes. The diagram is symmetric with no distortion. ✓

Limiting Case 2 — β → 1 (approaching light speed)

Expected: Both axes should squeeze toward the 45° light cone. At exactly β = 1 they would collapse onto it, making the coordinate system degenerate.

Observed: At β = 0.99 in the simulation, both axes are visibly compressed toward the light cone lines. The region between them narrows dramatically, consistent with the geometry. ✓

Connection to Anchor Question

The spacetime diagram makes the twin paradox geometric rather than mysterious. Alice's worldline is straight (vertical), while Bob's is kinked — he must change direction at the turnaround point. The straight worldline between two events always has the longest proper time (this is the relativistic analogue of "the shortest path between two points is a straight line," but inverted due to the minus sign in the spacetime interval). The diagram shows directly that Bob ages less not because of some force during turnaround, but because his path through spacetime is genuinely different from Alice's.
